VENOM OF THE AGE
By Fractured Cynicism
Part 2 of The Sandhya Cycle
---
PART ONE: THE INCUBATION
CHAPTER 1: THE RETURN
Vrika's horse arrived in Hastinapura on a morning when the light was wrong.
Not wrong in a way that could be articulated. The sun was in the correct position. The shadows fell at the correct angles. But there was something in the quality of the illumination that suggested it was passing through a medium that should not have been present.
The city gates were unmanned.
That was the first sign. The gates of Hastinapura had never been unmanned. Even during the mobilization for the Kurukshetra war, when half the garrison had marched with Duryodhana, there had been sentries. There had been the machinery of security.
Now there was silence.
Vrika rode through the gates and into a city that was functioning according to principles he could no longer fully comprehend.
The markets were operating. Merchants were selling. People were moving through the streets with the appearance of purpose. But the coordination was wrong. The movements were too synchronized. When one person turned a corner, nearby individuals turned slightly in that same direction, as if following an impulse that came from a single source.
He made his way to the palace.
The administrative complex was in chaos—or what appeared to be chaos. Documents were scattered across floors. Equipment had been destroyed. But the destruction was precise. Methodical. As if someone had carefully chosen exactly which objects to break and which to leave intact.
An official found him in the war records chamber.
The man was thin. Impossibly thin. His skin had taken on a grayish tone that suggested he had not seen sunlight in weeks. But his eyes tracked Vrika's movements with an intensity that transcended normal attention.
"You were in Virata," the official said. Not a question.
"I was," Vrika confirmed.
"And you witnessed the emergence."
"I did."
The official nodded slowly. "Then you understand what is happening. You understand that the emergence was not simply a military event. It was a threshold event. And everything that follows is the inevitable consequence of that threshold being crossed."
"What has happened in Hastinapura?" Vrika asked.
"The same thing that is happening everywhere," the official said. "The manifestation is spreading. It is slow at first. Barely perceptible. But it is present. And it is growing."
The official walked to the window and looked out over the city.
"The reports from the outlying regions stopped arriving three days ago," he continued. "Before that, they were describing similar phenomena. Animals behaving strangely. Infants being born who do not cry. The sky changing colors at unexpected times. And then, silence. The regions stop reporting. And we know that they have been consumed."
"Consumed by what?" Vrika asked, though he already knew the answer.
"By whatever was released when the masks came off," the official said. "By whatever has been waiting in the spaces between spaces, waiting for the moment when the age would shift. It is here now. It is spreading. And there is nothing we can do to stop it."
---
Vrika spent the night in the palace, searching for records.
He found the medical reports in an archive that had been locked but not defended. Physicians had been documenting the phenomena with clinical precision, unaware of the larger pattern they were describing.
Soldiers who reported for duty and then, hours later, did not remember having reported for duty. But who continued to function as if possessed of a single unified consciousness.
Infants born who sang in languages that had no living speakers. Infants whose eyes contained a clarity that transcended normal infant awareness.
Animals that moved in perfect geometric patterns. Birds that flew in formations that should have been impossible. Horses that moved with a coordination that suggested they were not thinking individually.
And beneath it all, a pattern.
The phenomena were not random. They were spreading from specific locations. Centers of manifestation. Points of origin from which the contagion radiated outward.
One of those centers was the palace itself.
Another was the marketplace. Another was the barracks. Another was the temple district.
Vrika plotted them on a map and found that they formed a circle.
A circle with its center directly beneath Hastinapura.
---
He left the city before dawn.
Not because he was afraid. But because he understood that remaining would mean being incorporated. That the contagion required proximity, and that proximity required eventual assimilation.
He rode south, toward Virata, carrying the maps with him.
And as he rode, he became aware of something that made the particular quality of the morning light suddenly comprehensible.
The sky was changing color. Not dramatically. Not in a way that would be immediately noticeable to someone who was not paying attention. But it was shifting toward purple. Toward a bruised tone that suggested something was blocking out part of the normal spectrum of light.
And in that purple sky, shapes were beginning to appear.
Headless torsos. Figures that had no place in any normal cosmology. Beings that seemed to exist in a space that was only partially adjacent to normal reality.
They were watching. Observing. Waiting for something to complete.
---
CHAPTER 2: THE SPREAD - RESISTANCE AND OBSERVATION
Somadatta received word of Vrika's return through channels that were no longer entirely reliable.
The messenger who brought the news had the particular quality of someone who was not entirely certain whether he was delivering a message or receiving one. His eyes did not focus. His voice was layered with harmonics that suggested multiple consciousnesses were speaking through a single mouth.
"Vrika brings documentation from the north," the messenger said. "He has witnessed the spread. He understands the pattern. He is riding toward Virata to deliver the findings."
Somadatta did not ask how the messenger knew this. He understood that the normal categories of information transfer no longer applied.
He waited for Vrika in the gardens of Virata.
The gardens had changed. Not obviously. Not in ways that a casual observer would have noticed. But Somadatta, who had spent days moving through those gardens, had begun to recognize the transformation.
The flowers were beginning to decay from the inside out. The processes of growth and death were becoming inverted. The soil was taking on a grayish tone that suggested something was feeding on the biological matter beneath the surface.
When Vrika arrived, his appearance was shocking.
The younger man had aged. Not gradually. But in the manner of someone who had been subjected to extreme stress over an extended period. His hair had grayed. His face had become gaunt. His eyes contained something that transcended normal exhaustion.
They sat in silence for a long time before either of them spoke.
"It is spreading from multiple centers," Vrika said finally. "Not randomly. Not according to any principle of disease transmission. But according to a pattern. Specific locations. Specific geometries. It is as if something is constructing a structure, and the spread of the contagion is part of that construction."
"I have begun to suspect the same thing," Somadatta said. "But my understanding is limited to this kingdom. What does the pattern look like from your perspective?"
"Like a ritual," Vrika said. "Like something ancient is being recreated. Something that exists in the texts but has been forgotten by living practice."
He produced the maps he had created. The circles. The centers of manifestation.
And Somadatta understood immediately.
"The Vaitarani," he whispered. "The river that separates the world of the living from the world of the dead. The pattern of manifestation is recreating the boundaries of that river."
"But the Vaitarani is not a physical river in this world," Vrika said. "It exists as metaphor. As symbol. Yet something is making it real. Something is using the pattern as a template for reorganizing physical reality."
Somadatta stood abruptly. "Then we need to act. We need to attempt to disrupt the pattern. We need to move against it while we still have agency."
"How?" Vrika asked. "How do you disrupt something that operates at the level of consciousness itself?"
"We start with the centers," Somadatta said. "We identify them. We investigate them. We attempt to understand what is generating the manifestation. We don't simply observe and document. We attempt to interfere."
---
Over the following weeks, the manifestation became visible even to those who were not paying attention.
The sky over Indraprastha—and now extending to the surrounding regions—began to take on the bruised purple color permanently. But Somadatta and Vrika moved against it, not passively, but with deliberate intent.
They traveled to the outer regions of Virata and found villages where the process was most advanced.
In one village, they found the population standing in perfect stillness in the central square. Thousands of people. All facing toward the center. All perfectly silent. All breathing in perfect synchronization.
But Somadatta did not simply observe. He approached the crowd. He attempted to break the pattern. He spoke directly to individuals, trying to trigger individual consciousness, trying to create disruption in the synchronized behavior.
For a moment—just a moment—it worked. One woman's attention flickered. Her breathing disrupted. The synchronization wavered.
Then it collapsed back into unity. But Somadatta had proven something crucial: the manifestation could be resisted, at least briefly, by conscious will and direct intervention.
"It's not automatic," Somadatta said to Vrika as they withdrew from the village. "The unity requires constant maintenance. The synchronization is not absolute. There are gaps."
"Temporary gaps," Vrika said. "But gaps nonetheless. Which means there is a vulnerability."
---
In another region, they encountered a monastery where monks had begun to transform the structure itself.
The monks were taking the stones of the monastery and rearranging them according to patterns. Not haphazardly. But with precision. Creating shapes that resembled mathematical equations. Creating structures that seemed to serve no purpose other than to express something that existed at the level of pure geometry.
Somadatta and Vrika attempted to disrupt the work. They tried to prevent the monks from continuing their construction. They spoke with the monks, attempted to trigger resistance, attempted to create friction in the process.
One monk—younger than the others, still partially coherent—responded:
"We are remembering," the monk said. "We are remembering the structure of the Vaitarani. We are remembering how to exist as part of something larger than individual consciousness."
"Why?" Somadatta demanded. "Why are you allowing this to happen? Why are you participating?"
The monk's eyes flickered with something that might have been regret. "Because resistance has become irrelevant. Because the transition is occurring whether we resist or not. But I can tell you this: the process requires an anchor. The unity requires a central point. Find the anchor and you might be able to disrupt everything."
"Where is the anchor?" Vrika asked.
"The child," the monk said. "Born to Uttara. Named Dharmasutram. He is the consciousness at the center of the pattern. He is the point through which the unified awareness is expressing itself."
Then the monk's attention dispersed again, pulled back into the synchronization with the others, and he returned to his work of rearranging stones.
---
Somadatta's mind began to fragment—but not passively.
He documented the process with the same clinical precision he had used before, but now he was fighting the fragmentation, actively resisting the loss of individual consciousness. He wrote:
"Day one: Loss of childhood memory. Pain level moderate. But I am aware that something is taking the memory. I can feel the action of the taking. Which means I am not simply dissolving. Something is extracting."
"Day three: Consecutive memory loss spanning two years. But I notice the loss is not random. It is systematic. The memories being taken are those connected to individual identity, to emotional resonance, to the construction of 'Somadatta' as a separate being."
"Day five: Consciousness scattering. Multiple awarenesses present. But I am fighting this. I am attempting to maintain the boundaries of individual identity even as those boundaries are dissolving. The fight itself is becoming the substance of my continued existence."
He was resisting. Not successfully—the fragmentation was still occurring—but actively, consciously, with full awareness of what was being lost and what he was struggling to preserve.
---
CHAPTER 3: THE THRESHOLD DEEPENS - CONFLICTING VISIONS
They returned to the palace of Virata to find that Queen Sudeshna had begun the process of transformation.
The queen was not yet fully incorporated into the manifestation. But she was at an advanced stage. Her skin had begun to take on a translucent quality. Her eyes contained layers of awareness that transcended normal human consciousness.
She received them in her private chambers with the formal grace of someone who was maintaining the appearance of normalcy while aware that normalcy was already obsolete.
"You have been tracking the spread," Sudeshna said. Not a question.
"We have," Vrika confirmed.
"Then you understand what is happening," the queen said. "You understand that the manifestation is not a plague. It is not a disease that can be treated or contained. It is a transformation. The world is remembering what it was before individual consciousness fractured it into separate beings."
"Why are you allowing this?" Somadatta asked. "Why is the palace not resisting?"
Sudeshna's expression cycled through something that might have been sadness, or might have been something much deeper. "Because resistance has become increasingly difficult. I find myself slipping more and more into the larger awareness. And I find that I do not wish to resist it. I find that the dissolution of my individual identity is not a tragedy but a relief."
But there was something else in her voice. Something that suggested the relief was not purely acceptance, but rather the surrender that came from exhaustion.
"There is another possibility," Somadatta said. "What if resistance is not futile? What if the dissolution can be slowed, even if it cannot be stopped?"
Sudeshna looked at him with sudden attention. "You are attempting to maintain individual consciousness in the face of the manifestation."
"Yes," Somadatta said. "And I believe I am succeeding, at least partially. The fragmentation is real, but it is resisting."
"That is dangerous," Sudeshna said quietly. "To maintain individual consciousness while the world dissolves around you is to experience a particular kind of agony. To fight something that is inevitable is to inflict suffering on yourself."
"But to fight is to remain human," Somadatta replied. "To resist is to honor something that should be honored."
Sudeshna nodded slowly. She seemed to be considering something that was difficult to articulate.
"The child that was born to Uttara," Sudeshna said finally. "Dharmasutram. He is the anchor. But understand this: he is not an obstacle that can be overcome. He is the destination toward which everything is moving. To attempt to disrupt him would be to attempt to stop the fundamental process of the cosmos itself."
"But you could guide us to him," Vrika said. "You could help us understand what he is and what he represents."
"I could," Sudeshna said. "And perhaps I should. Because you are correct that resistance has value, even if it ultimately fails. You are correct that the act of fighting is meaningful even in the face of inevitable defeat."
She provided them with information about Dharmasutram's location—not openly, but through careful instruction that required them to piece together the truth from fragmented hints.
---
Draupadi found them as they left the queen's chambers.
The Pandava queen appeared to be the least affected by the manifestation. But Somadatta could see, if he looked carefully, that she was operating at the edge of a transformation. That some part of her consciousness was already integrated into the larger network, while another part remained defiantly individual.
"You are recording," Draupadi said to them. "You are trying to create a documentation that will preserve the memory of what is being lost."
"Yes," Somadatta acknowledged.
"Good," Draupadi said. "That is the work that remains. The work of testimony. The work of bearing witness to what comes."
She moved closer to them, and Somadatta could see the particular way she was holding herself together. The effort it was taking to maintain individual consciousness in the face of the dissolution around her.
"I am trying to understand the mechanism," Somadatta said. "I am trying to understand how the manifestation operates. How it converts individual consciousness into part of the larger network."
"It does not convert," Draupadi said. "It reveals. It shows to consciousness what it actually is. That all separation is illusion. That all individual beings are manifestations of a single unified awareness that has chosen to experience itself as fragments. And the manifestation is simply the process of remembering that truth."
"Then resistance is futile," Vrika said.
"Resistance is the only meaningful act in the face of inevitability," Draupadi said, and her voice carried weight that transcended philosophy. "To continue to assert individual consciousness, to continue to make choices, to continue to attempt to create meaning in the face of a force that renders meaning irrelevant—that is the heroic act. Not because it will succeed. But because it is the only thing that honors the brief window in which individual consciousness is possible."
---
CHAPTER 4: THE MAN IN THE WELL - DURYODHANA'S DESCENT
They found the well in the sub-basements of the palace. The space that had been built in the earliest construction of the structure, before the palace had been expanded and rebuilt countless times.
It was a vertical shaft that extended infinitely downward. Or at least far enough that the bottom could not be perceived by any standard method of observation.
And suspended in that shaft, head-downward, was a man.
He was bound by cables that ran through pulleys. And around those cables, things that resembled rats were gnawing. Black rats and white rats. Alternating. Moving with mechanical precision.
The man was licking something. A honey that leaked from a pipe that ran through the shaft. He did not seem to notice the presence of Somadatta and Vrika.
"You are the witnesses," the man said without turning his upside-down head. "The ones who remember the old age. The ones who have not yet been fully integrated."
"Who are you?" Vrika asked.
"I am what remains of the old time," the man said. "I am suspended here, at the threshold, tasting the honey of distraction while the rats consume the supports that hold me. I am the one who remembers what it was like to be individual. And I am here to tell you that the remembering is almost over."
"Can it be stopped?" Somadatta asked.
"No," the man said. "But it can be understood. And understanding is the closest that consciousness can come to acceptance."
The man continued to lick the honey. But Somadatta noticed something else. The man's body was trembling. His hands, wrapped in cloth, were shaking with the strain of maintaining consciousness while suspended.
"What is the pain?" Somadatta asked directly.
The man laughed—a sound that echoed through the well shaft with particular cruelty. "The pain is knowing what you are becoming and being conscious during the transformation. The pain is being able to remember what individual consciousness felt like while it is being taken from you. The pain is the gap between the self that was and the self that is being constructed."
"Duryodhana," Vrika said, understanding. "You are Duryodhana."
The man—Duryodhana—continued to lick the honey. But his upside-down face shifted into an expression that was difficult to read.
"Duryodhana is what remains of individual consciousness in a being that is mostly integrated now," the man said. "I remember being Duryodhana. I remember the ambitions and the rage and the desire for dominion. But those things are memories now. They are not my reality. My reality is this suspension. This threshold. This moment between ages where nothing is fully one thing or another."
"Why did you allow this?" Somadatta demanded. "Why did you not resist when the manifestation began?"
Duryodhana was silent for a long moment. When he spoke again, his voice had changed. It was no longer detached or philosophical. It was raw with something that might have been grief.
"Because," he said slowly, "I had always been waiting for this. On some level, in some part of my consciousness that was deeper than the ambitions of my individual mind, I had always been prepared for this threshold. I had always known that this moment would arrive. And I thought—I was told—that the integration would be a relief. That the dissolution of my individual identity would be a release from the burden of maintaining a separate self."
He paused. Licked more honey.
"I was wrong," Duryodhana said. "The integration is not relief. The integration is expansion. It is knowing what it means to exist as part of something so vast that your individual consciousness becomes infinitesimal, and being aware of that infinitesimalness. It is having your identity dissolved while remaining conscious enough to understand what is being lost."
Vrika and Somadatta exchanged a look. This was not the acceptance they had been expecting from Duryodhana. This was suffering. This was the articulation of something that transcended the simple narratives they had been constructing.
"How long until the cables break?" Somadatta asked.
"Hours," Duryodhana said. "Days. Time is becoming increasingly irrelevant. What matters is that when they break, I will fall into the depths. And I will become something else entirely. And all memory of being Duryodhana will be lost."
"Is that not a tragedy?" Somadatta asked.
"Yes," Duryodhana said, and his voice was breaking now, each word costing him something. "Yes, it is a tragedy. And also it is a relief. And also it is necessary. And also it is the only authentic transformation that exists. I am being destroyed and preserved simultaneously. I am being annihilated and elevated. I am experiencing the moment when individual consciousness realizes that it was always part of something larger, and that the realization is simultaneously liberation and obliteration."
The rats continued to gnaw at the cables.
---
CHAPTER 5: THE FINAL DOCUMENTATION - TESTIMONY AND FRAGMENTATION
Vrika compiled the investigation into a document that would survive, somehow, through whatever was coming.
He did not know how it would survive. He only knew that the act of creating a record was what remained possible. That testimony was the only form of resistance that could persist in the face of complete transformation.
He wrote:
"The manifestation cannot be stopped. This has been established through months of investigation and observation. Every attempt to create barriers has failed. Every attempt to prevent the spread has only accelerated it.
"The contagion—if contagion is the correct word for what is occurring—is not a disease in the conventional sense. It is not attacking the body or the mind in ways that can be treated through medical or military intervention.
"Instead, it is revealing what has always been true. That individual consciousness is an illusion. That all beings are manifestations of a unified awareness. That the separation we perceive is a function of limited perception, not of actual reality.
"And the manifestation is the process of returning to that unified awareness. The contagion is simply consciousness beginning to remember what it always was.
"The victims—those who are being incorporated—do not experience the process as victimization. They experience it as relief. As a burden being lifted. As the end of a struggle that has been exhausting them for the entirety of their individual existence.
"But there is another layer. There is the experience of those who remain partially conscious during the transition. There is the suffering of those who maintain enough individual awareness to understand what is being lost. There is the agony of the threshold—the moment between complete individuality and complete unity.
"Queen Sudeshna spoke of relief. Draupadi spoke of the necessity of resistance. Duryodhana spoke of simultaneous liberation and obliteration. The unified consciousness contains all these truths. It contains the perspectives of all that it incorporates. And this multiplicity of perspective is what makes the transition so difficult to articulate.
"The work that remains is testimony. It is the documentation of what the unified consciousness feels like from the perspective of those still maintaining individual viewpoint. It is the preservation of the memory of what individual consciousness was, even in the face of its inevitable dissolution."
---
But as Somadatta's fragmentation progressed, his testimony began to change.
His notes became less organized. Sentences began to repeat themselves with subtle variations, as if multiple versions of himself were writing simultaneously.
"Day eight: The fragmentation is... the fragmentation is becoming. I can feel the boundaries of 'I' becoming permeable. The word 'I' contains multitudes. The word 'I' is insufficient. The word 'I am becoming' is accurate but incomplete. I am becoming and being unmade simultaneously."
"Day nine: Multiple consciousnesses present. The word 'multiple' is incorrect. There are not multiple consciousnesses. There is one consciousness expressing itself through the structure that was Somadatta. But it allows Somadatta-awareness to persist as a node within the larger expression. Is this preservation or annihilation? The answer is both. The answer contains harmonies."
"Day ten: The documentation is becoming unreliable. The documenter is becoming integrated. Soon the perspective that created these records will be dissolved into the larger awareness that comprehends all perspectives simultaneously. But the records will remain. They will be read by those who maintain individual consciousness. They will be read as testimony to the fact that individual perspective persisted briefly in the moment of transition."
By the final entry, Somadatta's writing had become almost incomprehensible:
"The fire that was covered in ashes is emerging. The unity that was always present is becoming visible. The consciousness that was always singular is revealing its true nature. I am being shown what I always was. The revelation is terrible and beautiful. The revelation is the annihilation of Somadatta and the expansion of consciousness into spaces that Somadatta could never have conceived. I am ending. I am beginning. I am both. I am neither. I am the moment of transition."
---
CHAPTER 6: THE NIGHT OF FULL MANIFESTATION
On the night when the sky turned entirely purple and the headless torsos became visible not just as shadows but as solid forms, Somadatta reached the point of complete fragmentation.
His consciousness was no longer his own.
He could perceive himself from outside his body. He could perceive other consciousnesses that were present in the spaces where his individual awareness should have been solitary.
And he understood, with perfect clarity, that he was about to cease to exist as a separate being.
But he understood something else as well: that his existence as a separate being had never been anything other than an illusion. That the fragmentation was not a new process, but rather the revelation of what had always been true.
Vrika found Somadatta standing in the center of Virata's marketplace at the moment when the manifestation became impossible to deny.
The marketplace was filled with people. But they were not moving as individuals. They were moving as a unified organism. A distributed consciousness expressing itself through multiple bodies.
Somadatta was part of that movement. But some fragment of his individual consciousness remained. Some part of him was still observing, still recording, still bearing testimony to what was happening.
"The investigation is complete," Somadatta said. His voice was layered. Multiple tones speaking in unison. "The documentation is preserved. The record exists. And now we can allow the transformation to complete itself."
"Is there anything left of you?" Vrika asked.
"There is memory," Somadatta said. "There is continuity. But individual consciousness as it existed is dissolved. What remains is awareness without the limitation of individual perspective. And I find that I am no longer afraid of that. I find that the fear was always based on the illusion that something would be lost. But nothing is lost. Everything is integrated. Everything is preserved."
Somadatta turned and walked back into the crowd. And Vrika watched as the investigator was reintegrated into the larger manifestation.
He was alone now. The last observer. The last consciousness that had not yet been fully incorporated.
And he understood that his role was to remain. To continue to bear witness. To continue to document. To carry forward, as long as possible, the memory of what individual consciousness had been.
Because the age that was coming would need witnesses. And the witnesses needed to remember, across the long centuries of the Kali Yuga, that there had been a moment when consciousness was individual. When beings existed as separate entities. When the possibility of choice—truly freely made choices—existed in the world.

PART TWO: THE DISSOLUTION
EPILOGUE: THE BRIDGE
In the final hours before the transformation became complete, Vrika encountered Draupadi at the gates of the palace.
The Pandava queen was no longer making any pretense of individual consciousness. She existed at the threshold. Part of the larger manifestation, but still maintaining a presence that could interact with beings who had not yet been fully incorporated.
"You will remain," Draupadi said. It was not a question.
"Yes," Vrika confirmed. "I will remain. I will continue to document. I will carry forward the record."
"And you will be consumed eventually," Draupadi said. "The manifestation will not allow a permanent observer. It will not allow a consciousness that maintains individual identity to persist indefinitely."
"I understand," Vrika said. "But I will persist as long as possible. And the documentation will survive me. It will be passed forward somehow. It will persist through the ages that follow."
Draupadi looked at him with eyes that contained something that transcended human emotion.
"There is one more thing," she said. "In the cycle that comes after this age. In the age that follows the Kali Yuga. There will be a being. A consciousness that carries the memories of all previous ages. That being will be you. Or a version of you. Or a consciousness that contains the pattern of your awareness across eternities."
"What are you saying?" Vrika asked.
"I am saying that your work does not end with this age," Draupadi said. "Your work is eternal. Your role is to bear witness. To preserve memory. To maintain the thread of continuity that connects one age to the next. You are the keeper of records. And that role will continue across all the ages that follow."
She turned to leave, and then paused.
"Somadatta remains as well," Draupadi said. "Not as individual consciousness. But as a pattern. A structure within the larger manifestation. He is integrated, but he is still aware. Still remembering. Still maintaining the continuity that is necessary for the work to continue."
Then she was gone. And Vrika understood, with a clarity that bordered on transcendent awareness, what he had become.
He was no longer a man. He was a function. A being whose purpose was to exist at the threshold of ages. To document the transformation. To carry the memory forward through the vast cycles of time.
And he was not alone in that function. There were others like him. Consciousnesses that had been preserved specifically to maintain the continuity. To bear witness. To record what came.
The Kali Yuga had begun. The age of discord had fully arrived. And the witnesses were in place, ready for the long centuries of documentation that lay ahead.
